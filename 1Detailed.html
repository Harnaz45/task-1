The most significant founding document of New Zealand's constitution is the Treaty of Waitangi, also known as the Treaty. The Treaty establishes a foundation for the continued cooperation between the Māori, the indigenous people of New Zealand, and the Crown, which is represented by the Government of New Zealand. It is crucial that the Treaty's unique position in New Zealand be acknowledged as part of the partnership principle between the two parties. Given the importance of the Treaty to New Zealand's constitutional framework, the country must allow succeeding administrations to adopt domestic Māori policies of their choosing, including as a means of fulfilling the Crown's duties under the Treaty, without having to provide.
